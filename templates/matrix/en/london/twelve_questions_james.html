James R

Profile and Leo's Take
Age：28　Years in London: 2

James was introduced to me by Luis. First of all, click on this link. It is the song James told me I should play for his introduction! Haha. Yeah, that was a first. Maybe you can tell from the song (and the pictures) but James is a music producer, and an amazingly talented one at that. For his shoot, he showed me around his studio and the complex which it’s in. The place was incredible, with a bunch of small office-like studios (rented out by a number of members in quite famous bands I won’t name here due to privacy) and a bigger recording studio. Basically a musician’s fantasy. James also had some of the coolest gear too which I could only dream of owning. OK, enough with the nerd talk.

James was even nice enough to have me over for dinner that night, and I actually joined him and his girlfriend Laura (also an amazing person and fellow food lover) who hold regular brunch parties by renting out little shops throughout East London. Way too cool. For his answers, James went all out with each one. Every single one is detailed, filled with examples, and full of substance. If I were to choose, I’d say thing and place (Q.3), thing (Q.7), children (Q.9), and dreams (Q.12). So see you around James, I’m sure we’ll meet again! Come to Tokyo WITH Laura this time and we’ll have something good to eat!!

Place for shooting and reason
Place: This is my studio in Homerton. Homerton is arguably, depending on who you are and what you do, the last affordable place in the borough of Hackney. Hackney was an area which was previously regarded as a hot spot for crime. It wasn’t a place you wanted to live in the year 2000. But the speed of gentrification, globally, not just London, has made this really the only affordable place in London. Even then…this room is small but you don’t even want to know how much money I spend every month on it…too much. But I make it work.

>>>You’re close to the station!

Yeah, I’m close to the station (laughs). And this is a good place to do it [the interview] because it’s quiet and not my flat. The best thing about this place is the fact that it’s not my flat, because my flat is full of music shit that my girlfriend hated (both laugh).

>>>I hope she likes your music though!

Q1
If you were handed US$2500 and received a one month vacation where you could live in one city, anywhere in the world, where would you choose? What would you do?

A1
Well, it’s funny you say that because, I’ve just come back from holiday which costs about that much and that was for two weeks (laughs). And the holidays were in San Francisco, Austin, New Orleans, Houston, and back.

>>>Whoa that’s an awesome trip.

It was a good trip. Yeah, yeah. And in light of that, if I were given it now… it would be one of two things. It would either be…I really want to go back to the States. I want to do New York, Detroit, Chicago, music towns on the opposite side. Or…to be honest, since I just came back from the United States, I’d say Europe for longer. Did you say two weeks was the limit?

>>>No no, one month!

One month!! Oh then Europe 100%. My money would go much further. Go to New York? Fuck that! (Laughs) I wouldn’t last a week! (Laughs) I’d want to go to Europe. Probably start off in…I’d like to go to the south of France. Go to Bordeaux, make my way down to Spain. Barcelona, Madrid, maybe Portugal. Then fly over to Italy. I’d go to the Amalfi Coast. I’ve only just discovered it, and I’ve been obsessed….there’s this TV show called “The Trip” they’re doing here. It’s these two comedians playing themselves, kind of this weird version of themselves, and they go on a trip to Italy, and they go to the Amalfi Coast. I’ve been obsessed ever since I’ve seen footage of it.

>>>It looks absolutely beautiful.

Have you been?? No?? Yeah, it looks insane. And then…I’d probably go back to Berlin, because I have loads of friends that live in Berlin.

Yeah, but even then, I feel it’s Germany, for me, there’s just something similar.



Q3
What one "thing" and "place" best represents your city?

A3
It depends whether I’m in a good mood or a bad mood.

>>>Can you give us one of each?

Yeah. The bad mood is Canary Wharf. Because Canary Wharf…well the people that live in Canary Wharf, and work in Canary Warf, it seems are determined to turn London into Dubai but with shit weather. I feel…well have you taken the DLR through Canary Wharf? No?? So the DLR is this kind of monorail that has no driver, which is really weird, and you sort of have this tour of capitalism. The monorail goes through the sky scrapers.

>>>Isn’t a lot of East London like that??

Yeah, yeah. It’s basically where all the banks are…well actually there is one good thing in Canary Wharf, and its Billingsgate Market. If you can muster yourself to get up early enough, I recommend you go there. It’s a fish market, and you can get amazing fish there. But yeah, other than that, Canary Wharf is a testament, sort of display of capitalism, I think. That would be my bad mood.

In a good mood, I would say Peckham in South London. Peckham is still…I think the vibe you feel in Peckham is still probably the vibe you felt in Hackney 10 years ago. I’m going to a party in Peckham tonight at my friend’s house. All my friends live in Peckham. If I moved down here on my own volition and I was single, I would definitely choose to live in Peckham. The rents are affordable…actually the rents aren’t that different here, but you get more for your money, I guess. Say if you were paying 600 quid [slang for pound] a month here, you would be living in a small room. If you’re paying 600 quid a month in Peckham, you’d have a large room with a garden.

>>>Wow. [NOTE from James: In even just the last half year, the rent in the same rooms in Peckham has risen to 700~800 pounds]

And Rye Lane in Peckham is just buzzing. For example I’m going to get on the train to go down there after this, and I know for a fact that it will be buzzing. Friday night. I’ll be getting on from Dalston Junction to Peckham Rye, and it will just be buzzing. Tonnes of young people. The streets are alive.

The thing is, I moved here because of my girlfriend and work, and I have to say I love Hackney. It’s…if you like the things she likes, like going out to have good food, good bars and everything, it’s really really great.

>>>Yeah, the Hackney, Shoreditch area has been incredible!

Yeah it’s great. But if you were young, an artist, and wanted something with a little more edge―Peckham. And Peckham, you know, gives me a little bit of hope. I see the parties held down there, the people down there―it’s got the most animated people. It’s really got its own thing going on.

Q6
How do you feel about the future? Both yourself and mankind.

A6
It’s interesting because…well there’s this really really good interview that I was reading the other day, and it was about this artist who actually summed up the answer to that question really well. His name is Matthew Herbert. Absolutely amazing producer, one of my favorites, a classical house producer. He’s worked with big bands, all kinds of music.

>>>Could you give us an example?

Well…he does a lot of his own stuff. He hasn’t really produced other bands. He made an incredible concept album revolving around the life and death of a pig. Like all the stuff I showed you with samplers, he’s the Don of all that. And going back to the interview, in it he talks about his latest album, in which he’s dealing with the concept of his own personal future, which he’s so positive about. He’s got a young kid, a stable career, everything that he really wants. But he’s also aware that his happy existence is on top of a system that is so grossly unfair. And he’s made a concept album about that.

>>>Wow…very interesting.

Yeah, really interesting. I haven’t heard anything of it yet, but if you just search “Matthew Herbert live school interview” you’ll find it. Posted last week [as of May, 2015].

And so regarding this, my thoughts…honestly, I think my life is going well. I live in a nice flat with my girlfriend, And I love her, and we are having an amazing time living together, so that’s going well. And I have this studio, this room, and yeah, sometimes I may have to mix a track that I don’t really like, but in the grand scheme of things, it’s a great existence. Honestly, I spend everyday here, in this room, and after I finish teaching, I work on a personal project. And I’m hoping, optimistically in the future, that it will pull through. So everything is good.

But in the general scheme of the world, I feel terrible. Yeah, I just…it’s just…going to America was an eye opener. Particularly…just…I think so many people have got it wrong over there. That thing that’s going on in Baltimore is just fascinating [referring to the riots going on at the time]. Have you seen “The Wire”? Yeah? Have you read the article on “The Two Americas”? By David Simon?? It’s the best thing of writing that you’ll read. It was in The Guardian, and it was taken from a speech he gave in Australia. “The Wire” is all about “The Two Americas”; one, the one you were probably brought up in, where you go to a decent school and go to college and all that, and the other, where America is in the habit of dismissing people. The idea of “The Loser.” I think it’s just an infectious and obnoxious thing.

And it’s not just America. It’s the world. We’ll just discard people, like that. I don’t know. I feel like things are getting worse in a lot of places, and the the human race is regressing, which I feel is a scary thing. And we’re living on the tip of it.

At the same time, I’m benefitting from the system, and living quite well. I struggle to articulate it…I’m sure Matthew Herbert’s album will be better than what I just said. Listen to his record (laughs).

[Starts reading writing from record]…So as Newsweek recently reported, when a newly born baby returns home for the first time from the hospital, the parents quickly discover that the howling does not always come from hunger, fatigue, or a wet diaper. And people are having kids so much later now, people are being more selfish, and not necessarily in a bad way. It’s just now…you can prolong the time where it’s just about “you”. Maybe it’s a byproduct of capitalism. I mean…look at my parents…my parents were, well my mother was 24 when she had me. Holy hell. You think about people having kids at 24, that’s really young. And that’s it. That’s the first thing that pops into my head.


Q10
What would be the ideal thing to see the first moment you wake up? What would be the ideal thing to see the moment before you fall asleep?

A10
You know what, the ideal thing would be, if we’re talking senses, would be silence. I have tinnitus [link], and I haven’t experienced silence for a year. Because right when you fall asleep, that’s when tinnitus bothers me the most. I mean it doesn’t affect me at all right now with all this background noise. It’s one of those things…I can get over this but yeah, I have tinnitus because I’ve been a fucking idiot and turning up the volume too loud. All this deejaying.

>>>Yeah, deejaying…

Yep, deejaying with really loud monitors for long amount of times. Yeah, all these fucking stupid decisions. It’s just regret really, every time you hear that ringing noise. It’s a broadband now too, not just high frequencies. I’m a sound sleeper, but if one day, I woke up and my tinnitus was gone—which has happened to some people so I’m praying—that would be ideal.

When I go to bed…yeah the really sappy answer. My girlfriend. I really like falling asleep next to her.

(Laughs) Yeah we were staying near Dolores Park so we went there many times before, and we wanted to try the other one. So we went to Golden Gate Park and we bought a joint for a dollar. And I hadn’t been smoking too much weed recently. I smoked a lot when I was in Liverpool, but here, I’ve got stuff on (laughs). Too much stuff on, I don’t really have the time. And you only really smoke weed when you’ve got nothing on (laughs). And then we stumbled into a bar, in Height Ashbury…

Yeah, and it’s also totally cheesy and crappy (laughs). It’s like Camden. People go there with like a guitar over there shoulder and it’s like, “Dude, you got the memo 50 years too late. You’re a food guy right…[starts searching on phone]… oh I’ve been telling this to everyone…here it is. “Jerk spiced duck hearts, with pickled pineapple and thyme salt” - the best thing I’ve eaten.

Yeah it was on a little kebab skewer, and it was amazing. But the best time was right after that when we had pizza by Dolores Park. It was the best pizza I’ve ever had. Just cloud 9. I’m stoned, it’s a warm evening, I’m with Laura, I’m eating this amazing pizza, and I had a great day. And it was supposed to be the crap day too, we had nothing planned. I know I know (laughs). I’m not a subscriber. I fully expected that when I got there it would be 100 dudes with bongos on a hill or something, but it was more like 4000. It was insane.

>>>Terrifying moment?

(Thinks)…I don’t know. It wouldn’t be anything recent.

Yeah, but those are really just nerves…not really terrifying. Terrifying is when you have a threat or danger to yourself or loved ones…so ok. We go back a few years. Back when I was in Liverpool and I came home to my flat to discover that it had been burgled. It was a slow realisation. That shit me up so much, because it would have become from something where I got my computer stolen and stuff, to something that would have been in the fucking news. Like, really gruesome thing. So yeah, that’s probably the most terrified. Other than being a kid and being terrified of all kinds of shit (laughs).


Q12
What was your childhood dream? What would you say to the childhood you, now? And what is your dream now?

A12
s Funny enough, childhood dreams is like when you want to be the coolest guy in the neighborhood or something?

>>>That works!

Ok…childhood dream was probably to be…in a band. Probably. Actually it depends on which part of childhood. When I was really younger I wanted to be an artist. I was really into drawing. When I got older, I wanted to be an actor for a bit, because I was into drama. And then it was music. It was music from 12 onwards. That’s what I wanted to do…anything that I was asked to do was twisted into music. That was like a dream.

What would I say to my childhood me? “The people you admire are not gods. They just got their shit together. And you can too.“ The biggest problem about being brought up in Liverpool, in my opinion, if you are into artistic pursuits, is you’ve never met anyone who has succeeded in it. So it just feels so unattainable. All my favourite bands were London bands. I was just hooked on them…my favorite band was Bloc Party. I was obsessed with them. And actually I now know Gordon Moakes [former bassist and founding member of Bloc Party] quite well.

>>>That’s awesome!! I loved “Silent Alarm”.

Yeah, Paul Epworth, the producer, huge, you know for Adele and Florence and the Machines, did three records that year. He did “Silent Alarm” for Bloc Party, he did “Capture/Release” for The Rakes, and he did The Futureheads debut album, which I can’t remember the name of. Yeah, that just turned me on. That was the most exciting shit in the world for me, at the time. And the other band that shaped me massively was Radiohead, and they probably got me into electronic music. I still listen to their records in awe. How fucking good they sound. So yeah, I would say “Don’t put all those people you admire on a massive pedestal. They just got their shit together.”

And the more I get into music, making records, and the more I get into the mechanics of it, I’m just like…it doesn’t make me…I still appreciate those records. But, once you understand the mechanics, it becomes, “Yeah it’s really good, but I know how you did it.”

>>>Yeah, that I think is everybody’s first step in music production.

Yeah, yeah, exactly. It’s like…it’s not a dark art. It’s difficult to do well, to be really good is difficult, but…yeah, still in some instances I’m like “Wow. I really don’t know how you did that.” I listen to Burial, I listen to Aphex Twin, I’m like “Fuck me. I do not know what you did there”. “Rhubarb” by Aphex Twin. “I don’t know how you did that…” It’s synths, but how did you make the sound like that, you…you clever guy.

And my dreams now. I’ve got…I want to…in my life, I want kids. I want to live happily ever after. I want to stay living in London for a bit…and maybe move on to somewhere else. Before it becomes too much like Dubai (laughs). And music wise, I want to release techno records. And I want to produce artists. Sit at the mixing desk, and make people sound amazing. That’s the general theme for me.
