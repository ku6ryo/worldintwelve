Gautier Jacquemain

Profile and Leo's Take
Age：26　Years in Paris: 6

Gautier I was introduced at one of Simon’s gatherings with his friends. Gautier is plainly put, a thinker. He is the type of person I imagine was walking around Paris 150, 100, 50 years ago, spending time in the cafes with like minds, discussing philosophy, politics, literature, art, music, and various forms of ideologies. He holds the spirit of Rousseau, Balzac, Bastille, Picasso, Sartre, Diderot, and the countless others that roamed and strived in the city of love. Many of the French people I met (and really all the people I met in Paris) were the thinker type (which is why I love the city and its people, even though Parisians get a bad rep), and Gautier was a defining one. He answered the 12Qs with a process, one in which I felt he was breaking down each question into logical parts, then provided the deep thought that tackled each one. It was one of those occasions where I was thinking just as much as Gautier who was answering the questions. So obviously, I would like readers to take a look at all of them, but if I were to recommend a few, they would be the images (Q.5), future (Q.6), important thing (Q.7), and dreams (Q.12). So thanks Gautier, I know we’ll meet again, and next time, even more deep conversations shall await us!!

A6
(Thinking)… I’m very worried about climate change.

>>>You were talking about carbon footprints, so yeah.

Yeah. Well, it’s something like… it’s a meta problem. It’s irreversible and it lasts forever. Or at least as far as the humans can conceive time, it’s gonna be forever into next ice age but.

>>>Until it all crumbles.

(Laughs) Yeah. Right. And I’m worried about it because it takes reciprocity, you know? And we don’t have a worldwide government that can impose measures on us, and the conference and stuff, it’s been a failure for the last 20 editions. [This interview was conducted before the COP21 in Paris in November, 2015]

>>>Yes.

And I think that change has to come from citizens. It has to come from civil society, because if we wait for the governments to move, then we’re done for. And I used to have that idea that it could all end up as a major apocalypse, like with food shortages and people starting to fight each other, and all that very dark imagery. But I also think that history can change so fast and if it can change so fast as to cause an apocalypse, then it can change as to cause major awareness. People are becoming aware of stuff.

Look at what happened with the migrant refugee topic in France. There’s this huge move of solidarity across Europe although it’s not dreamland. It’s not hegemonic. There are also counter-opinions and voices but what has happened is great. It’s like miracles at once. Conservative right wing, liberal Merkel saying at once, hey, I’m the master here. I’ll do what I want. Let’s welcome thousands and thousands of refugees so I was like, yeah. Shit yeah. (Laughs) So maybe there’s just no longevity in the movement, but only the fact that it can happen punctually and the demonstration being quite spontaneously organized in Paris, even with a few thousand people attending. That’s it. That stuff’s going on. So hope is love. And I don’t even know whether one should be pessimistic of it or optimistic, one should be pragmatic and just try to do the very little they can. The very little they’re able to do, or whatever they’re able to contribute. To struggle on that climate change topic. [Speaks French] What one has to do is to do the very, very little they’re capable of and that’s it. Wow, I’ve been talking too much.
