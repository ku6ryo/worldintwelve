Maja Fyfe

Profile and Leo's Take
Age：40 (but 20 at heart :)　Years in Stockholm: 3

Maja was introduced to me through Enni, where they both met at the Swedish language school (run by the government, naturally), as new citizens of Sweden. Maja is a mother of two, and working at the national TV and radio station in Stockholm. Talking with Maja was one of those situations I’ll remember for a long time; personally experiencing situations where countries have gone through war, immigration (a hot topic in Europe as of Sept. 2015), and financial collapse, her life history is just so incredibly different from any person so far. Details will come in her 12 Questions, but I was just aghast throughout the interview. I had read and thought about such situations, and these were significant reasons for me starting this project, but hearing personal stories was on a completely different level. For the answers, I recommend the future (Q.6), partner (Q.8), moments (Q.11). and dreams (Q.12). So Maja, thanks again for taking time out of your day, and please do bring your sons to Japan one day! That’ll be another adventure haha

Q6
How do you feel about the future? Both yourself and mankind.

A6
>>>This is very interesting…especially thinking of where you come from.

When you come to this stage, when people say mid-life crisis, you do try to question “oh my god, what have I done? Oh, the youth is gone, what’s coming ahead?” You feel changes in your body. You become more tired. You feel “oh, my back hurts...oh my god, everything is going downhill from here.”

These are the questions that I had in mind last year when I was 40, but now you kind of think “okay, but you need to look ahead and try to make the sense of your life”. And what I figured, I think it’s best to try to live in the moment every day. Take one day, look forward to little things. Because if you try to think about big picture a lot, then you get “oh, god…” you’ll get depressed.

Trust me, when I was younger I never thought about it, but now at this stage, you do. So it’s best to take one little step at a time. Focus on happy, little things in your life every day and little things to look forward.

>>>Wow that’s really interesting…do you have an image for just in general, for humankind?

Oh, humankind -- doomed. [laughs]

>>>Oh, that’s too bad!!

Yeah, because you see news and you see horrible things, you sometimes lose faith in humanity. I just pray though that maybe it will be alright. I come from Bosnia, where I was born. There was a lot of injustice, people suffering. It’s repeating and repeating itself all the time. Corruption, politicians —I’m not optimistic. That’s my opinion.

Q8
What do you look for in a partner?

A8
I think very important thing is that you click and be yourself with that person, and you know straight away that you met somebody that you don’t need to pretend anything. You don’t need to be anybody else. When I met my husband, I was myself straight away.

>>>So upon first contact.

Yeah, you just feel yourself. …we clicked on many levels. And the way you know if you’re going to marry somebody, you just know - you have common view in many things -- “how are we going to raise children?" for example.

My husband is Catholic, from Northern Ireland, and I am a Muslim from Bosnia—so it’s crazy. [Both laugh] These days, you don’t hear things like that. It was very important that we think the same -- “hey, who cares, we’re all human beings, we’re all one and we all have the same god. Who cares if we don’t do this or that”. We raise our children to be proud of who they are and they can choose whatever they want, but we’re not going to impose anything on them...

So that’s important that these important matters, you click, you agree on things, what you’re gonna do, view of life. Otherwise, there are gonna be problems later.

Q11
What was the happiest moment in your life? What was the most terrifying moment in your life?

>>>What was the most terrifying? Anything you can share with us?

Well, I’m trying to think because there are obviously the times, yeah, during early ‘90s when the war was happening. And I’m watching the news and I knew it could be my family there because they live five minutes away.

And you don’t know. And that “not knowing.” --I remember my friend ringing me, “I have the list of dead people.” I just couldn’t, I said “ohhhhhh”, I started screaming. My roommate said “okay, it’s okay, don’t…”

Yeah, these moments like that.

Q12
What was your childhood dream? What would you say to the childhood you, now? And what is your dream now?

A12
Yeah, it’s hard because I’ve never--some kids know exactly “oh, I want to be that.” And I don’t know, that’s a tough one to answer because I don’t have anything in particular. I suppose I wanted to be happy. I always cared about my family and that we’re always together and things like that. …So yeah, I never as a child could dream of what actually happened in my life. I always thought it would be more predictable -- my home, and my family, and my town and all that, and being happy.

But looking back now, everything what happened, I think I could still say "yeah, I wish it didn’t happen" because who would wish war to happen, but then it makes you always stronger and—oh, what it did to my perspective and experience as a human being. To put it like that, I have a lot to pass to my children, how to fight out when things get tough, how to rise up and all that, so I would say to that child, [laughs] “don’t worry, you’ll be very strong person later.”

>>>Wow, that’s such a great message. And do you have any goals or dreams now?

Just stability in life. I don’t want to move anywhere else. I don’t want to learn another language.

>>>As we know. [laughs]

No, some stability. Finally, I got a job, we are settling in Sweden. Just you know what my only goal is stay healthy, that’s most important thing, good health. That’s what I wish for. Everything else, it sorts itself out, trust me.
