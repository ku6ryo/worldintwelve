Round 1


Round 1: Everybody is a little nervous, as this was the first round of the test city. Everything was new. The three members put their treasure on the table. Jay couldn't make it because of a cold, so he sent pics via email.

Susan: PASMO (Metro Card)

  • Coming from Australia, it was only recently that an efficient travel and money system using cards was installed.
  • The hectic, fast-paced life style of Tokyo is highlighted through its transportation system.
  • It's something I always have in my wallet. Everyone has one, its an essential part of daily life. Like brushing your teeth.
  • The scanner where you touch the card has been researched and placed at an angle which is most efficient. Its not flat.


Jay: Map

  • Every treasure hunter needs to own a map. Gladly technology becomes handy these days.
  • Offline city map+GPS positioning has been a great help in my aimless and never ending walks.
  • In the same time allows me to pin new discoveries: vintage record stores, yummy eateries or intriguing locations to revisit and photograph.

Aimi: Fake Eylashes

  • Originally derives from the GAL culture in Shibuya, but now is a common item for all girls.
  • You don't see people in other countries wearing them.
  • The eyelashes make your eyes look bigger, an obvious indication of the complex held by Japanese girls towards their eyes.
  • Each one is intricately and delicately designed, in a very Japanese way.


Leo: LED Lightbulb

  • Tokyo is a city that never sleeps, and that can be seen through all the lights that are on 24/7.
  • However, there are efficiency and ecological problems with the normal lightbulb.
  • The LED lightbulb was invented in Japan, and all the lights in the city are slowly being switched to these.

Round 2


Round 2: After a successful round one, the participants are a little more relaxed. Conversations take place more freely.

Susan: Free Tissues

  • You don't see them in Hong Kong or Singapore. A brilliant way of advertiseing.
  • To advertise on everyday items--like fans in the summer--is something very Japanese. Although you do start to get desensitized...
  • You never have to buy tissues again, and simultaneously saves you time because you don't have to go to the convenience store.
  • There are busineess that revolve around the free stuff, like cases for these tissues.


Jay: "Tokyo" A Tsujiko Noriko Song

  • Part of the album "From Tokyo To Naiagara" https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RvnTrlb9HEk
  • It dates back 11 years, still long before my 1st visit to Tokyo (8 years ago).
  • I have been into electronica genre of music, Tujiko Noriko album and Aoki Takamasa she collaborated with.
  • Discovered it through my Japanese-Polish girlfriend at that time.

Aimi: The "Tokyo Hiyoko"

  • One of the famous souvenirs of Tokyo, but is actually from a company in Fukuoka.
  • The company sold them as Tokyo gifts at the 1964 olympics, and have been a Tokyo favorite ever since.
  • That pettiness is something I portray of Japanese haha
  • And the fact that, in the end, no one really cares is also very Tokyo-like haha


Leo: Company Name Card Holder

  • Truly an indicative item of the Japanese salaryman
  • By wearing it around your neck, you are appealing to the world, "I work for this company".
  • The fact that its around the neck is significant. Corporations in western countries have name cards too, but they usually don't resemble a leash haha

Round 3


Round 3: Each member is starting to roll. Then Susan presents, puts a new light on a side of Japanese society, and Aimi and Leo are aghast...

Susan: Mask

  • Most people who have never been to an Asian country hold negative connotations towards these masks.
  • However in Japan, many people where them to warm up, for hayfever, or just to hide the fact they aren't wearing make up.
  • It also proves how the Japanese work too much. In Australia, you can easily "check a sicky" and call in sick for work.
  • But in Japan, people go to work wearing masks, even if they feel a little sick. And everyone around them will wear them too, for prevention.


Jay: Shochu

  • In general as it's so unpopular in the west and so much unlinked to Japan.
  • Everyone thinks Japan = Sake when talking about alcohol.
  • Yet again stereotypes clash with the reality.
  • I like this brand for its lovely label design.

Aimi: A superball with a panda inside

  • This has more to do with the place I bought it than the thing itself: Ueno, with its park, zoo, and museums.
  • Pandas are the bear of China, but when it first came to the zoo in Japan, everbody tried to make money by merchandising off of it.
  • And almost all the merchandise is made in China, which is ironic. It doesn't even matter!! haha
  • （Everyone's opinion）When the panda was about to have a baby, it was national news. It's all really a part of this obsessive fad culture of Japan.


Leo: Maccaron Bubble Bath Mix

  • First of all, the Japanese love baths.
  • But making bubble bath mix in the shape of a maccaron=very Japanese. They like making everything look like food.
  • Then there is the "Kawaii" culture, and the ever prevalent idolization of western culture mixed in too.
  • Baths + Food + Kawaii + Western = The perfect Japanese girl object haha

Round 4


Round 4: The final round. Each member takes out their final object.

Susan: Sounds of Nature (via iPhone）

  • You can hear "sounds of nature" throughout Tokyo
  • In many cases they are artifical sounds.
  • Since it is such a metropolis, everyone seems to have a longing for nature.
  • Like the "Otohime" in the bathrooms, Japanese people might be very sensitive to sound.


Jay："No Purpose" Book

  • Somehow the reason I came here or rather an excuse.
  • The body of work that consumed last 6 months of my life here and allowed me to explore cities day to day vibe, practices, pace; far from stereotypical hectic living.
  • The book consists of 64 images.

Aimi: Psyllium

  • Originally from the idol concerts held in Akihabara. The fans would hold the colors of their favorite idols.
  • However these psyllium sticks can now be seen at every and any concert.
  • There are many different types of sticks, from longer lasting ones with weaker light, to 5 minute bright sticks meant for one song only.
  • Hardcore fans use the different types depending on occasion.


Leo: Incense

  • Tokyo is a surprisingly scentless city for being so big. However, if you go to Asakusa, you can smell incense.
  • Japanese like "no smell". There is even a deoderant with the smell, "no smell".
  • People don't wear much perfume either.
  • However the one smell that I feel is Japanese is the smell of incense.
