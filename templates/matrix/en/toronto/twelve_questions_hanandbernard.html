Han Dang and Bernard Chan

Profile and Leo's Take
Age：Han: 35, Bernard: 38 　Years in Toronto： Han: Over 10, Bernard: All his life

Han and Bernard were introduced to me through Steven. I had dinner with them one night at a nice restaurant, and Han was super nice and invited me to her birthday party. Han’s birthday party was truly “Toronto” with every mix of race, nationality, and type of person attending. I remember I had a very thought provoking conversation with a half-Indonesian, half-African friend, after talking with an Indian British woman, followed by a Polish and Canadian couple. Crazy. Han and Bernard are also a true Toronto couple, with each being of different Asian descent, and having cool, free thinking opinions on a variety of topics (as you’ll see in the answers!). It was a lot of fun talking with them, and Bernard (or B, as we call him), gave me some of the kindest words I’ve received regarding my project. As for the answers, this was the first and last time where its a combination of two different interviews put into one, with Han and Bernard answering each question separately. It ended up being vary interesting, because you can directly compare the similarities and differences—almost like one of those Buzzfeed articles that compares genders and generates a lot of hits haha. Read whichever one interests you!! (I personally liked the flag (Q.4)) Anyway Han and B, thanks so much for everything, and we’ll meet up the next time you come to Tokyo!!

Q9
Do you want to get married? Do you want children? What do you want to do with them? How do you want them to grow up?

A9
>>>(Knowing previously from Steven that Han and Bernard made the decision not to have children) Could you explain your decision?

HAN: Yep. It was, you know, like a long, hard decision for us because we decided we had to access, you know, why do you like children, right? Like, “do we want children because we were expected to have children?”. You know, the cycle of life, right? You’re born, you go to school, you get married and you have kids, you work until you’re 65, and then you’re sick and too old to do anything with all that money that you have. So we… I had to search myself. “Why do I want kids.” Is it because of pressure from my family or is it something that I really want? I’ve never felt that I have to have children of my own. I never felt that I need to become a parent to be happy. I felt that I am fulfilled and satisfied with other things in my life that I have going on. And we did talk about how if we ever wanted children, we can always adopt because I truly believe that we are able and capable of loving other kids just as we would love our own. So we don’t need to have blood- related kids, so...

Also, another big thing for us was that I value freedom. I value flexibility, and having kids really ties you down, because you have to be structured. You can’t pick up and travel if you want to. You can’t… do whatever you want, because then you become the kid, right? Like everything surround your child because that’s your responsibility. So for me, one of the things that’s important for me is… freedom. And it’s hard to balance freedom and raise kids at the same time. Not that it’s impossible. But it’s a choice that we were willing to make.



BERNARD: Oh, yes. So we decided not to have children because we just didn't really want them. It's almost like asking somebody, "What do you want on your pizza?" Certain people say, "Hey, I want mushrooms." Some people don't. It's just really a preference. Nothing against people having children. It's just one of those things, we didn't want to do. And we didn't feel… We didn't want to fall into the pressure because parents around you will definitely pressure. Most of the parents will pressure you to have children. Friends will pressure you. Other people will pressure you because it's almost the norm. You go to school. You go get a job. Go get a place, car, get married, have children. And then do whatever you're supposed to do 'til you're 65 or whatever, retired. Retire and hopefully live the life that you dreamt about when you were probably 12 or something and you're pretty much near death. And then the cycle goes on. So, we decided, “Hey. You know what? We love our life right now. We love our lifestyle." We found the freedom to do whatever we want. And we decided we don't need a child. That was it.
