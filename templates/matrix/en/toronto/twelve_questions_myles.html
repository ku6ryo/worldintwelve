Myles Drayton

Profile and Leo's Take
Age：22　Years in Toronto： 21.5

Myles was introduced to me through Steven at the Japanese language exchange at University of Toronto. Myles met me another day for some serious board gaming--something of which I hadn't done in ages and I realized I should do much more often. (Some of the games we found there were awe-inspiring!) Myles just being himself is my definition of Toronto; multiethnic (Barbados x Phillipines), multilingual (English, French, Tagalog, and Japanese), and open to diversity and variety. He also gave a couple of the most memorable answers to the questions (coming from me as I'm writing this, I've done over 60 12Qs). The two that have stuck with me even now is his children (Q.9) which I've quoted his answer many a time now just in regular conversations with people, and his moments (Q.11) which really got me thinking deeply. His answers to the most important thing (Q.7) is really cool too. So Myles, thanks again, and I'll try to meet up with you in a non-capital city in the future!! haha

Q6
How do you feel about the future? Both yourself and mankind.

A6
…In general the future scares me. And I don't know if that's just because there are so many dystopian movies out right now that you just kind of have the feeling that everything's going to go terribly. But I think even looking around, I think with just how fast technology's developing, I think it's getting a little bit scary… to look around and see six year old kids on iPads who spend more time with their iPads than with their friends. I think that's a little bit discomforting to me. I don't exactly know what that means for the future. It could just mean that we become a society where we interact more through a screen than we do in person. There are… there are people who would be, “Oh, that detracts from just human interaction in general”. I don't know if I fall into that camp. I think you can interact with a person through a screen. I don't have much of a problem with it. But I think it would just be a very different world. I think technology is going to shape things to be just completely different from what we've seen before. Even going to the Philippines, which is a lot less developed than Canada, it was a bit of an eye-opener to see how… just how different things can be over just a couple of years.

Q9
Do you want to get married? Do you want children? What do you want to do with them? How do you want them to grow up?

A9
Marriage: Yes
Children: Yes

So in an ideal world, I would like to be independently rich so that what we can do whatever we want (laughs). But I think first of all,and something that's really important and dear to me is that my children would be multilingual. And I think, I don't know if that's a product of doing an undergrad in linguistics or just being surrounded by different languages my whole life. But I think, I think one of the most interesting facts that I learned in linguistics is that monolingualism is actually very rare in the world. It's limited to a very small set of countries and the majority of countries in the world have inhabitants that speak two, three, four languages on a daily basis. And so I'd like my children to be able to do the same. I don't know if I have the ability to be able to teach my children that many languages, but I would like to. And then with that, I would like to travel around the world with them. I don't think it really matters where we go, I'd just like them to experience what life is like in other places, because I do think we are very pampered in Canada and we're very oblivious to how pampered we are until we go somewhere else and see how they live.

Q12
What was your childhood dream? What would you say to the childhood you, now? And what is your dream now?

A12
So I had a lot of childhood dreams. I wanted to be a lawyer originally and that was at the suggestion of my mom because I would always argue with her and I would always win the arguments. And she was just like, “you should be a lawyer because you like arguing, and you're pretty good at it”. But, yeah I know nothing about law at this point. And so (laughs), and actually on that same note, I would probably go back and tell my younger self “most arguments aren't worth it”. You need to learn when to stand down and just say okay and move on with life. It's not… it's not worth it most of the time. At another point I wanted to be a doctor, but then I realized I didn't really like blood. I'm not afraid of blood I just don’t—I don't like sick people, which is really weird. I'm always afraid I will do something that will kill them, so… I don't like that kind of setting, so I decided not to be a doctor. And then for awhile I wanted to be a musician and then, i was like there's no money in that, so I didn't do that either. And then for awhile I was lost.

But right now I'm heading for speech language pathology. That is my current dream, which is kind of weird because it's very similar to a doctor and I am working with sick patients all the time. Or at least I'm volunteering right now with sick patients all the time. But yeah, that is my current goal, that is what I'm working towards. I don't really… I'm not the type of person to plan very far ahead, so that is my current goal. I've already applied, so I'm just waiting for a result.
